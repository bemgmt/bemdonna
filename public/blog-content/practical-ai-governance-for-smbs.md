# Practical AI Governance for SMBs

## Introduction

Most SMB teams adopt AI for speed. The real challenge appears later: who is accountable when automation acts across customer communication and internal workflows?

Good governance is not overhead. It is what makes scale safe and sustainable.

---

## Governance Starts with Permission Boundaries

Define exactly what automation can and cannot do:

- Allowed: acknowledge inquiries, route requests, schedule meetings.
- Conditional: send policy-sensitive responses with approval.
- Restricted: legal advice, clinical guidance, financial commitments.

Written boundaries reduce risk and improve team confidence.

---

## Role-Based Access Is Non-Negotiable

Use role scopes that match operations:

- Admin: integration and policy configuration.
- Manager: workflow approvals and escalations.
- Agent: queue handling and response execution.

Do not grant broad access for convenience. Access should follow responsibility.

---

## Logging and Auditability

Every meaningful action should be traceable:

- Who triggered the action.
- What was sent or changed.
- When it happened.
- Why escalation occurred.

Audit history is essential for disputes, compliance checks, and internal quality control.

---

## Human-in-the-Loop Done Right

Human review should trigger automatically for:

- High-value commitments.
- Sensitive policy or legal language.
- Low-confidence classifications.

Human review is not a failure state. It is a governance control.

---

## Operational Checklist

- Document allowed, conditional, and restricted actions.
- Configure role-based access and least privilege.
- Enable timestamped logs across communication and workflow actions.
- Define escalation thresholds and approval paths.
- Schedule monthly governance reviews.
- Run incident drills for response and rollback.

---

## FAQ

### Is governance only for enterprise?
No. SMBs need governance early because teams are lean and mistakes are costly.

### Does governance slow operations?
It reduces avoidable risk while preserving speed through clear policy automation.

### How often should policies be reviewed?
Monthly for active workflows, quarterly for broader controls.

---

## Conclusion

AI delivers value when trust and control are built in from day one. If your governance model is explicit, automation can scale without creating unpredictable operational risk.

Introduction

AI adoption in small and mid-sized businesses often starts with urgency.

Faster response times. Lower administrative workload. Better lead handling.

What frequently gets overlooked is governance.

Governance is not a corporate formality. It is the operational structure that ensures AI systems behave predictably, securely, and within compliance boundaries.

For SMBs, the goal is not complexity. The goal is controlled deployment with measurable oversight.

What AI Governance Actually Means

AI governance for SMBs should focus on five pillars:

Role-based access control

Auditability

Escalation boundaries

Data handling standards

Compliance alignment

Governance is not about limiting capability. It is about defining guardrails.

An operational intelligence layer like DONNA must be permissioned, logged, and reviewable.

Role-Based Access: Preventing Internal Risk

Not every employee should have identical permissions.

Define:

Who can activate automation?

Who can modify workflows?

Who can access customer transcripts?

Who can escalate financial actions?

Role-based permissions prevent accidental misuse and create clear accountability.

For example:
A sales rep may trigger follow-up sequences, but only a manager may modify escalation rules.

This structure reduces internal risk.

Audit Logs: Visibility Without Micromanagement

Auditability is essential for:

Regulated industries

Customer dispute resolution

Internal performance review

Legal protection

A compliant AI system should log:

Timestamped actions

Channel used

Trigger source

Escalation decisions

Human overrides

With DONNA, every interaction across voice, SMS, email, and chat can be logged in a centralized activity stream.

Transparency builds trust internally and externally.

Human-in-the-Loop Controls

AI should not operate without boundaries.

Define thresholds for:

Financial transactions

Contract changes

Legal language

High-risk escalations

When risk level exceeds defined parameters, the system should:

Pause automation

Notify designated human

Require approval

This preserves operational efficiency while maintaining oversight.

Data Handling and Infrastructure Standards

AI systems must adhere to modern cloud security standards:

Encryption in transit and at rest

Secure API integrations

Access token management

Multi-factor authentication

Secure cloud hosting environments

For SMBs operating in healthcare, legal, or finance, additional regulatory compliance considerations may apply.

Governance ensures these requirements are addressed before scale.

Governance Checklist for SMB AI Deployment

Use this checklist when implementing AI workflows:

Define role-based access permissions.

Document automation boundaries.

Enable full audit logging.

Establish escalation approval triggers.

Confirm encryption standards.

Align with industry-specific compliance requirements.

Conduct quarterly governance reviews.

Structure prevents chaos.

FAQ
Is AI governance only necessary for regulated industries?

No. Every business benefits from defined access control and auditability, even if not legally required.

Will governance slow down operations?

Properly configured governance accelerates adoption because teams trust the system.

What compliance standards apply?

Depends on industry. Healthcare may require HIPAA alignment. Finance may require additional data protection controls.

Can governance be added later?

It is more effective to implement governance from the start rather than retrofitting after workflows are live.
